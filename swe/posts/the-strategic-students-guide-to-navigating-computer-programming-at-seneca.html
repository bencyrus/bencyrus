Let's start with a bit of advice:

There Are Advisor Limitations: Navigate Your Own Path


While advisors can provide guidance, they might not have all the answers or have wrong ones. Trust, but verify what they say.

Understand Your Student Archetype and Course Load Flexibility

There are generally three types of students in the Computer Programming Program (CPP):
  • The Fast Trackers:
    These students aim to complete the program quickly by taking a heavy course load. Good news - there's no cap on the number of courses you can enroll in, provided you meet the prerequisites!
  • The Steady Goers:
    These students prefer a balanced approach, not too fast yet not too drawn out. They follow a more traditional path through the semesters.
  • The Slow and Steadies:
    These students prefer a lighter course load. They may have other commitments, such as work or family, that prevent them from taking more than 3 or 4 courses per semester.

Start Strong: Divide and Conquer

Before you start your first semester, you should have a good idea of what courses you'll be taking and when based on your student archetype. Check the Scheduling tool to find the real pre-requisites for all of your courses. Will tell you more about each course later.
You should also understand the weight of each task in your courses so you can plan strategically.

Keep In Mind:

  • Course Maps and Prerequisites Are NOT Accurate

    Contrary to what you may think, the official course map provided but the school is not accurate. It identifies some pre-requisites that are wrong, and does not tell you how else you can arrange your courses.
    First Semester Insight:
    The suggested courses are IPC144, APS145, CPR101, ULI101, and COM111. However, what's not explicitly stated is your freedom to choose any electives (ELS) without prerequisites. Feeling ambitious? You can opt for more than the recommended five courses, but caution is advised during the initial semester.
    The Truth About Prerequisites:
    Not all solid lines (indicating prerequisites in the course map) are accurate. For instance, though PMC is listed for the 4th semester, it doesn't actually require prior courses! Strategic course selection can potentially enable you to complete the CPP program in just three semesters or have a very light last semester to focus on your job hunt.
    Last Semester FAQ:
    • Q: Is it ok if I don't have a full-time course load in my last semester?
      A: Yes, you can take any number of courses you like in your last semester. Even one course is fine. It won't impact your graduation or immigration status.

  • You May Encounter Hurdles

    Failing a course isn't the end of the world, but it requires smart recalibration of your plan. I recommend taking IPC144 and OOP244 in your first two semesters and not failing them since they are pre-requisites. However, if you do, that doesn't mean you have to take an extra semester. You will just have to move a few courses around to make up for it.

    Scenarios to Consider:

    • Failing IPC144:
      If you stumble with IPC144, retaking it becomes your priority. Since this setback restricts you from advancing to OOP244, Web211, or SFT221, you can still maintain the course of your program and graduate within the 4 semesters by taking IPC144, DBS211, EAC594, and PMC115 as well as your electives. Since EAC594 and PMC115 are much less time-consuming than OOP244, WEB211, and SFT221, taking all of your electives to balance the load over your semesters is advisable.
      Although a 7-course semester looks scary, I would argue this combination is actually easier than your regular second or third semester. If you still are not comfortable with 7, take 6 and one elective in your third semester.
    • Failing OOP244:
      Failing OOP244 can complicate your journey, but it's not a roadblock to your four-semester plan. This misstep means OOP345, DSA456, and almost all professional options are off the table for now. However, you can keep pace by opting for DBS311, WEB322, SYD366, and any remaining electives or courses like PMC115 or EAC594 if you haven't taken them yet. Brace yourself, though; a hiccup with 244 can make your final semester a bit harder, but nothing too crazy.
      Your fourth semester would typically feature heavy hitters like OOP345, DSA456, and a Professional Option, along with any electives or leftover courses. Playing it smart means you've already tackled some, leaving just these critical few. It's about strategic course management — taking the right subjects at the right time keeps you on track, even when there are bumps along the way.
      Note: Course map and your advisors say that OOP345 is a pre-requisite to DSA456. That is incorrect. OOP244 is and even my professor was surprised to learn that.
    • Failing OOP345:
      A hiccup with OOP345 isn't a showstopper — seriously, no need for panic! Unexpectedly, OOP345 isn't the gatekeeper it's often made out to be. Sure, it's a bummer, but it doesn't spell doom for your four-semester timeline. You still have a buffet of professional options to explore that don't list OOP345 as a prerequisite. So, your last semester might look like this: retake OOP345, add on DSA456, and throw in whatever's left on your plate. Quick fact: OOP345 isn't actually a pre-req for DSA456, so that's a bonus! Keep your chin up and your strategy flexible.

General Tips

  • Critical Early Assignments:
    Prioritizing initial assignments, labs, quizzes, and workshops is crucial for two key reasons. Firstly, these early tasks often carry simpler objectives, providing a more accessible opportunity to secure high grades. These act as a buffer, potentially offsetting any future shortcomings or difficulties with more complex tasks. Secondly, the foundational knowledge laid out in the first half of the semester is indispensable for understanding more advanced concepts introduced later on. Thus, a solid grasp of these basics paves the way for a smoother, more successful learning trajectory.
  • Real Pre-requisites for Courses:
    Contrary to popular belief, not all courses have strict prerequisites. Here's a quick rundown to help you navigate:
    • Most electives: No prerequisites — take them anytime!
    • PMC115: No prerequisites, but be persistent in enrolling as it often prioritizes last-semester and CPA students. You might snag a spot eventually.
    • IPC144, APS145, CPR101, ULI101, COM111 or 101: No specific pre-requisites.
    • OOP244: Requires IPC144 AND APS145.
    • WEB222: Needs IPC144.
    • DBS211: Either IPC144 OR ULI101. Remember the "OR"!
    • SFT221: IPC144 is a must.
    • EAC594: Either COM101 OR COM111.
    • SYD366: IPC144 AND either COM101 or COM111.
    • OOP345: Just OOP244.
    • WEB322: Necessitates WEB222.
    • DBS311: DBS211 is the gatekeeper.
    • DSA456: Requires OOP244.
  • The Udemy Advantage:
    Don't dismiss Udemy! It's a perfect supplement to your learning. Think of it as a time-saver, not extra work. Courses often drop to $15-20 when you create a new account. I personally have many. Or you can just have 1 account and contact support. They will give you 90% discount. Code along with the tutorials; don't just assume you know the content.
  • Experience Doesn't Equal Ease:
    Just because you've programmed before doesn't mean CPP will be a cakewalk. Even if you have years of experience in languages like Python, IPC144, OOP244, OOP345 might offer a unique challenge. And that is not just because they are in C and C++; Seneca has make them very demanding. I personally found it tougher despite having 3 years of programming experience in other languages.

Course Specific Tips

IPC144: Introduction to Programming Using C

Recommended Udemy Course:
C Programming for Beginners
I recommend taking it before school starts but it's not necessary.

Study Tips:

  • Don't solely rely on your course professor. Even forget you have one and supplement your learning with the Udemy course and aim to study ahead of the class schedule. Really learning this course can save your time massively in the future.
  • Each week, cross-reference the upcoming course topics with the Udemy course and study in advance.
  • Try to complete the Udemy course based on the order of your college curriculum for the most aligned experience.
  • Don't worry if you feel dumbfounded or feel like you don't understand anything! That's normal.
Note: When you learn it from Udemy first (which goes into more depth than your prof) then when the prof explains, you totally get it! This way you spend 2-4 extra hours a week but I promise you, it saves you more than 10 hours a week when doing the workshops and final project!

Extra Tips:

  • Prioritize the first few workshops, as you may find the latter ones more challenging.
  • Don't let pointers and file reading intimidate you. Seek out additional resources, hire a tutor, or join study groups to grasp these concepts better. If you learn them, you will find OOP244 much easier than I found it!
  • Unless you have enough time and really want to practice IPC144, do not enroll in a class with Prof. Cameron Gray due to potential workload disparities. He doesn't hesistate to fail half of the class if needed.
  • For a comprehensive learning experience, consider watching Fardad Soleimanloo's IPC144 lectures on YouTube.

OOP244: Introduction to Object-Oriented Programming Using C++

Recommended Udemy Course:
Beginning C++ Programming - From Beginner to Beyond

Study Tips:

  • Approach this course similarly to IPC144: supplement your class lectures with the Udemy course. I personally would watch the Udemy course before the semester starts, or at least be ahead of the class schedule in watching the course. You may not have time to watch it in the second half of the semester.
  • There are a few concepts that can confuse you. Constructors and destructors, pointers, file reading, operator overloading are some of them. My advice: Do not panic. Just look them up on YouTube or watch relavant video from the Udemy course or Fardad's lecture. Just try to understand what they even are before beginning to code. Helps a lot. Most people quit because they struggle to understand so many new terms and concepts. You can find Fardad's lecture by clicking on the following link: Fardad's OOP244 Playlist

DSA456: Data Structures and Algorithms

Recommended Udemy Course:
Master the Coding Interview: Data Structures + Algorithms

Study Tips:

  • You need your professor in this course since Seneca goes deeper in the course outline than any tutorial I could find online. Still, the Udemy course added covers most of the material you need.
  • I notice that many students get really confused with the time and space complexity and get overwhelmed. It is one of the easiest parts. You can learn it within an hour or two on YouTube or through the Udemy course added. Make sure you do it.
  • Important Note: The content of this course is the questions you will be asked in almost every software job interview. Make sure you learn it before, throughout, or after the semester because you have to anyways.
